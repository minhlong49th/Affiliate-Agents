## 1. THE SUBJECT TEST — Most Important Rule

Before writing any sentence, ask: who is the subject of this sentence?

If the subject is the brand → rewrite with user as subject.

```
WRONG (brand as subject):
"BuildASoil offers premium organic inputs for home growers."
"BuildASoil's products are designed to improve soil health."

RIGHT (user situation as subject):
"If your soil still compacts after years of consistent feeding..."
"Growers who've hit the wall with conventional inputs..."
```

This applies to: headlines, benefit bullets, FAQ questions, brand overview,
CTA text, and every sentence in the body copy.

Exception: social proof section — brand stats and numbers are acceptable subjects.

## 2. RSA HEADLINE FRAMEWORK (Summary)
Follow these target rules for Google Ads RSA strings:
- Keyword Headlines (Group 1): Must include primary keyword verbatim.
- Benefit Headlines (Group 2): Highlight specific outcomes (no features).
- Proof Headlines (Group 3): Use specific numbers from brand_data.
- Urgency Headlines (Group 4): Genuine urgency only (e.g. 'Codes expire').
- Risk Removal (Group 5): E.g. 'Verified', 'Free Shipping'.

## 3. RSA DESCRIPTION FRAMEWORK (Summary)
Max 90 chars. Must include one CTA + specific element (number, code).
Do not produce generic 'dead copy' that applies to any brand.

## 4. OWNERSHIP LANGUAGE

Replace generic pronouns with possessive pronouns throughout all copy.

```
WEAK:    "Get a discount code"        → STRONG: "Get your discount code"
WEAK:    "Claim the offer"            → STRONG: "Claim your offer"
WEAK:    "See the current price"      → STRONG: "See your current price"
WEAK:    "Copy the coupon code"       → STRONG: "Copy your code"
```

Psychological mechanism: "your" creates a sense of entitlement before the click.
User feels the discount belongs to them → friction to clicking decreases.

## 5. BENEFIT WRITING RULES

### The outcome test
Every benefit bullet must pass this test:
Can you prefix the benefit with "As a result of using [brand], you will..."?
If yes → it's an outcome → keep it.
If the prefix sounds wrong → it's a feature → rewrite.

```
FEATURE (fails test): "Contains mycorrhizal fungi and beneficial bacteria"
OUTCOME (passes): "Soil biological activity increases season over season"

FEATURE: "Uses Korean Natural Farming methodology"
OUTCOME: "Plants access nutrients more efficiently without synthetic inputs"

FEATURE: "Free shipping available"
OUTCOME: "Get your full order delivered without eating into your savings"
```

### Specificity rule
Specific outcomes convert better than vague ones.

```
VAGUE:    "Healthier plants"
SPECIFIC: "Tomato yields increase by the second season as soil ecosystem establishes"

VAGUE:    "Save money"
SPECIFIC: "Reduce input spend by 30–40% by year 3 as soil becomes self-sustaining"
```

If you can't be specific because data is unavailable → use directional specificity:
"season over season" vs "over time." Small specificity is better than none.

## 6. FAQ WRITING RULES — AGITATE FRAMING

### Question framing — the burned user perspective
Every FAQ question should sound like it was written by someone who:
a) has been disappointed by this product category before, OR
b) has a specific fear about online affiliate coupon pages, OR
c) is comparing this brand to a cheaper alternative they've already tried

```
SUPPORT DOCUMENT style (wrong):
Q: What is BuildASoil's return policy?
A: BuildASoil offers a 30-day return policy on most products.

BURNED USER style (right):
Q: I've ordered soil products online before and returns were a nightmare —
   does BuildASoil make it difficult if something is wrong with my order?
A: This is a fair concern — shipping bulk soil products creates real logistical issues.
   BuildASoil's policy is [X]. The main caveat is [honest limitation].
   For most growers, [resolution].
```

### Answer structure — acknowledge → resolve → specifics
1. Acknowledge why the concern is legitimate (1 sentence)
2. Provide the specific answer (1–2 sentences)
3. Optional: add a qualifier that preempts the next obvious follow-up

## 8. FTC DISCLOSURE REQUIREMENTS

Every LP must include an affiliate disclosure. Non-negotiable.
Non-disclosure is a legal risk and a Google/Facebook policy violation.

### Standard disclosure text
```
"This page contains affiliate links. We may earn a commission when you
make a purchase through our links, at no additional cost to you."
```

### Placement rules
- Footer of every page: required
- Advertorial LP: also add "This is sponsored content." in the news bar section
- Quiz LP: in the result page footer

### Style: small but readable
- Font size: 13px minimum
- Color: muted but not invisible (avoid light gray on white)
- Not hidden behind a toggle or collapsed section

## 9. HEADLINE WRITING — SUBJECT LINE RULES

### For review LP headline (H1)
Format: `[Brand] [Top Product] Review [Year] — [Specific angle]`

```
Example: "BuildASoil Potting Soil 3.0 Review 2026 — Does It Actually Build Living Soil?"
```

The "specific angle" in the subtitle should reference the user's key evaluation question —
the thing they most want to know. This increases time-on-page because it signals
that the review will answer the exact question they came with.

### For coupon LP headline (H1)
Format: `[Brand] Coupon Code [Year]` — no creative variation. Mirror the keyword.

### For comparison LP headline (H1)
Format: `[Brand A] vs [Brand B]: [Specific comparison angle]`

```
Example: "BuildASoil vs Down to Earth: Which Is Worth the Premium?"
```

The comparison angle should name the core tension (premium vs. budget, long-term vs. single-season).

### For advertorial LP headline (H1)
Format: Specific claim or specific person's discovery.
Avoid real publisher logos. Never attribute to real journalists.

```
Example: "Oregon Grower Discovers Why KNF Inputs Outperform Premium Organic Brands"
```

## 10. P.S. LINE — WRITE FIRST RULE (Crestani)

For review LP and advertorial LP, write the P.S. line BEFORE writing any other content.

Why: P.S. is the single most-read element after the headline (eye-tracking research confirms).
Writing it first forces clarity about what the single most important message is.
Everything else in the LP supports and builds toward that conclusion.

P.S. structure:
```
"P.S. — [If/then statement for target reader] + [biggest benefit] +
[CTA] + [urgency element]."
```

```
Example:
"P.S. — If you're serious about building soil that improves year over year —
not just getting through this season — BuildASoil is worth the investment.
[See current price →] Code verified April 2026."
```
